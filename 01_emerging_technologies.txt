1. ALTERNATIVE LIVESTOCK FEEDS TO NOURISHMENT

Purpose:

The primary goal of this technology is to convert organic waste materials, such as food scraps, agricultural by-products, and industrial waste, into nutritious feed for livestock. This reduces waste, lowers feed costs, and contributes to more sustainable and circular agricultural practices.
Usage:

Education: In educational settings, this technology is used to teach students about sustainable agriculture, waste management, and innovative approaches to feeding livestock. It is also a topic of research in agricultural and environmental science programs.
IT: The IT sector plays a role in optimizing the process through data analytics, monitoring systems, and automation technologies. IT systems are used to track waste sources, optimize feed production, and ensure quality control.
Examples:

Education: Universities and agricultural schools may have research projects or pilot programs where students study and develop alternative livestock feed from waste products. For example, a project might involve converting brewery waste into cattle feed and analyzing its nutritional value.
IT: Companies like AgriProtein use IT systems to manage the production of insect-based feed, which involves converting organic waste into high-protein feed. These systems track the efficiency of waste conversion, monitor environmental conditions, and ensure the consistent quality of the final product. Additionally, smart sensors and IoT devices are used in farms to monitor the health and growth of livestock fed with alternative feeds, providing real-time data for optimizing feed formulations.


2. CARBON-CAPTURING MICROBES

Purpose:

The primary goal of carbon-capturing microbes is to mitigate climate change by removing carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the atmosphere and converting it into useful by-products, such as biofuels, biomass, or other valuable compounds. This technology aims to reduce greenhouse gas concentrations, contributing to a more sustainable environment.
Usage:

Education: In educational settings, carbon-capturing microbes are studied in environmental science, microbiology, and biotechnology courses. Research focuses on enhancing the efficiency of these microbes and exploring their potential applications in carbon sequestration and sustainable energy production.
IT: In the IT sector, computational models and data analytics are used to optimize the conditions for microbial carbon capture. IT systems monitor microbial activity, analyze data from experiments, and simulate large-scale applications of these microbes in different environments.
Examples:

Education: Universities may conduct research projects to engineer microbes that can capture carbon more efficiently or to explore the potential of different microbial strains in sequestering carbon. For instance, students might study how cyanobacteria can be used to convert CO₂ into biofuels.
IT: Companies and research institutions use IT to model the behavior of carbon-capturing microbes in various ecosystems, optimizing their deployment in carbon capture and storage (CCS) systems. For example, systems might simulate the efficiency of carbon capture in industrial settings or track microbial performance in bioreactors designed for large-scale CO₂ absorption.


3. ELASTOCALORICS: THE COOL NEW WAY TO HEAT AND COOL

Purpose:

The primary goal of elastocalorics is to provide an energy-efficient and environmentally friendly method for heating and cooling by utilizing the temperature changes that occur when certain materials are mechanically stressed. This technology aims to reduce reliance on traditional refrigeration systems, which often use harmful refrigerants and consume significant amounts of energy.
Usage:

Education: In educational settings, elastocalorics is studied in materials science, physics, and engineering courses. Research focuses on discovering and optimizing materials with strong elastocaloric effects and developing practical applications for heating and cooling systems.
IT: In the IT sector, computational simulations and modeling are used to predict the elastocaloric properties of materials, design efficient systems, and optimize their performance. IT also supports the development of control systems for elastocaloric devices.
Examples:

Education: Universities and research institutions might conduct experiments to identify new elastocaloric materials or improve the efficiency of existing ones. For example, students may explore the potential of shape-memory alloys to be used in elastocaloric cooling systems.
IT: Companies developing elastocaloric cooling systems use IT to model the behavior of materials under stress, simulate the performance of cooling devices, and optimize designs for commercial applications. An example is a startup developing elastocaloric heat pumps that reduce energy consumption in residential and commercial buildings, monitored and controlled by IT systems for efficiency.


4. EMERSIVE TECHNOLOGY FOR THE BUILT NEW WORLD

Purpose:

The primary goal of emersive technology is to create fully immersive digital environments that blend the physical and virtual worlds, enhancing how people design, interact with, and experience built spaces. This technology aims to revolutionize architecture, construction, and urban planning by enabling more intuitive, real-time visualization, simulation, and collaboration.
Usage:

Education: In educational settings, emersive technology is used to teach architecture, urban planning, and design through immersive experiences. Students can explore virtual models of buildings and cities, simulate real-world conditions, and collaborate on projects in shared digital environments.
IT: In the IT sector, emersive technology is used to develop and optimize virtual reality (VR), augmented reality (AR), and mixed reality (MR) platforms. These platforms are applied in the design and management of smart cities, construction projects, and real estate, providing detailed simulations and interactive models.
Examples:

Education: Architecture schools use VR to allow students to walk through digital models of their designs, experiencing the space as if it were real. For instance, a university might employ AR to overlay historical architectural features onto a present-day cityscape for educational tours.
IT: In real estate, companies like Matterport use emersive technology to create 3D virtual tours of properties, enabling potential buyers to explore homes remotely. In urban planning, tools like Microsoft's HoloLens are used to visualize proposed infrastructure in a real-world setting, allowing stakeholders to assess the impact of new developments before they are built.


5. RECONFIGURABLE INTELLIGENT SURFACES (RIS)

Purpose:

The primary goal of reconfigurable intelligent surfaces (RIS) is to control and manipulate electromagnetic waves, such as radio and light waves, to improve the performance and efficiency of wireless communication systems. RIS technology aims to enhance signal strength, reduce interference, and optimize the use of the wireless spectrum, enabling more reliable and faster communication networks.
Usage:

Education: In educational settings, RIS is used in courses and research related to wireless communications, signal processing, and electromagnetic theory. Students and researchers explore how RIS can be used to design smarter communication environments, optimize wireless networks, and support emerging technologies like 5G and 6G.
IT: In the IT sector, RIS is employed to improve the performance of wireless networks by dynamically adjusting the reflection and transmission of signals in real-time. IT systems use machine learning and optimization algorithms to control RIS and enhance connectivity in various environments, from urban areas to indoor spaces.
Examples:

Education: Universities may have research projects that investigate the potential of RIS to boost signal coverage in challenging environments, such as dense urban areas or rural regions with limited infrastructure. For instance, students might experiment with RIS to optimize signal distribution in smart buildings or on university campuses.
IT: Telecom companies are exploring the use of RIS to enhance 5G networks by strategically placing these surfaces in urban areas to improve signal quality and coverage. For example, RIS could be used in a stadium to ensure that all spectators receive a strong signal, regardless of their location, by intelligently directing and reflecting wireless signals throughout the venue.
